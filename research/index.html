3. Accurate and Smooth Motion Control

3.1) Design, Analysis, and Experiments of Preview Path Tracking Control

How to achieve smooth, accurate, and computationally efficient steering control? We address this issue by developing a discrete-time preview steering controller, which considers vehicle dynamics and can smoothly deal with future road curvature. Its tracking performance is comparable to MPC but the computational load is greatly reduced due to its closed-form control law. Experiments on an automated Lincoln MKZ validated its improved tracking accuracy and smoothness.

  • Details

3.2) Safeguard Protected Preview Lane Keeping Control

How to bound tracking errors to a certain safe level for better safety? In this paper, we solve this challenge by designing a preview lane keeping control supervised by a safeguard controller. The safeguard controller intervenes in the preview control if and only if the tracking error is approaching the safety boundary. The proposed controls were implemented on a self-driving vehicle platform and tested on open roads and in the Mcity test facility.

3.3) Preview Path Tracking Control With Delay Compensation

Does your self-driving car run with unexpected slalom and steering oscillation? If yes, then one potential cause is the system time delay and steering lag. This paper targets this challenge and presents a delay-compensated preview steering control design, which can lower tracking errors and significantly improve stability margin compared to controls ignoring delay and lag. All the controller design, performance analysis, and experimental validation on a self-driving MKZ platform are detailed.

  • Details

  • Control considering delay and lag

    This video shows our field tests of the designed preview lane keep control considering the input time delay and steering lag. The steering operation is smooth without oscillation and overshoot, which demonstrates the improved system stability margin.

  • Control ignoring delay and lag

    This video shows our field tests of the benchmark control, a preview lane-keep control ignoring the input time delay and steering lag. In the first 25 seconds, the vehicle aimed to run at 40 mph but failed because of the increasing oscillations, which finally led to instability. Then the vehicle speed was reduced to 27 mph. The system became stable, but the oscillations were still significant. Compared with the first video, this one highlighted the benefits of considering system delay and lag in lane-keeping control design, i.e., it significantly improves stability margin and allows for higher cruising speed.

3.4) Design and Test of Speed Tracking Control for the Self-Driving Lincoln MKZ Platform

How to smoothly and accurately track a given speed considering the powertrain nonlinearity? Taking the Lincoln MKZ as an example, we designed two computationally efficient, smooth, and accurate speed-tracking control algorithms, i.e., 1) an optimal preview control if future information (reference speed and road slope) is provided a priori, and 2) a PID+C control if the information is unavailable. All controls are validated by the MKZ platform at Mcity.

2. Building Hardware Platform and Software System for Self-Driving Vehicles

Launched by Prof. Huei Peng at 9/2016, supported by Mcity, this OpenCAV project aims to provide a reusable and sharable self-driving testing platform to researchers in UofM and Mcity member companies. Xu serves as the core researcher of this project from 10/2016.

Xu has hands-on experiences in the hardware platform of the Lincoln MKZ, e.g., activate all sensors using ROS/Polysync, develop data recording/conversion software, set up distributed computing framework, develop DSRC/5G based V2X drivers and a by-wire control interface for preventing unsafe commands. Apart from the hardware platform, the aforementioned algorithms and software he developed for robust and intelligent decision making, path planning, and motion control are also deployed.

1. Eco Speed Planning for Self-Driving Vehicles with Hybrid or Conventional Powertrains

How to drive the vehicle with minimal fuel consumption? During Xu’s Ph.D. project, he designed speed profile optimization algorithms and powertrain control strategies for self-driving vehicles with hybrid electric powertrain or conventional powertrain. Nonlinear constrained fuel-optimal control problems are formulated and solved by Legendre pseudo-spectral method to parse the eco-driving laws. If you are interested in how to use minimal fuel (energy) to accelerate a car, cruise on highway (for both conventional vehicles and HEV), and drive on varying slopes, please refer to the following papers for detailed algorithms, performance, and the underlying mechanisms.

  • Eco-Driving on Varying Slope
  • Eco-Cruising of Parallel HEV
  • Eco-Cruising of Conventional Vehicle
  • Eco-Accelerating
  • Pseudospectral Computation of Eco-driving Problem
